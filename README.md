# Evidence-Based Control Testing

## 📌 Overview

This repository outlines a structured methodology for testing security controls using an evidence-based approach.

It is designed to ensure that control assessments are consistent, transparent and audit-defensible across third-party assurance and internal review activities.

---

## 🎯 Purpose

The methodology is intended to:

- Validate security controls against defined requirements 
- Ensure evidence is sufficient to support assurance conclusions 
- Enable consistent control testing across multiple suppliers 
- Support audit-ready documentation and traceability 
- Reduce subjectivity and interpretation in assurance 

---

## 🔍 Control Testing Approach

The control testing lifecycle follows a structured sequence:

1. Define the control objective 
2. Establish “what good looks like” 
3. Identify required evidence 
4. Assess control design 
5. Assess operating effectiveness 
6. Record results and supporting evidence 
7. Identify gaps and limitations 
8. Agree remediation actions 
9. Report outcomes 

---

## ✅ Control Assessment Criteria

### Control Design Effectiveness
- Is the control formally defined and documented? 
- Does it align to the control objective? 
- Is ownership clearly assigned? 
- Is the control implemented appropriately? 

### Operating Effectiveness
- Is the control operating as intended? 
- Is it performed consistently over time? 
- Is there verifiable evidence of execution? 
- Are exceptions identified and addressed? 

 ---

## 📊 Evidence Evaluation Principles

Evidence used to support control testing should be:

- Relevant to the specific control requirement 
- Current and within scope of the assessment period 
- Complete (not partial or selective) 
- Traceable to a defined source 
- Independently verifiable where possible 

 ---

## 🧾 Common Evidence Types

- Policies and standards 
- Standard operating procedures 
- System configuration screenshots 
- Access control records and reviews 
- Vulnerability management reports 
- Incident and problem management records 
- Audit logs and monitoring outputs 
- Governance forums and meeting evidence 

 ---

## ⚠️ Evidence Limitations

Common evidence limitations that should be clearly documented:

- Outdated documentation 
- Sample-based evidence without coverage explanation 
- Screenshots without context or timestamps 
- Missing linkage to control requirements 
- Evidence of control existence without proof of operation 

---

## 🚦 RAG Assessment Model

- **Green:** Control is effective, supported by sufficient evidence 
- **Amber:** Control is partially effective or evidence is incomplete 
- **Red:** Control is ineffective or evidence is insufficient 

---

## 🔗 Link to Assurance Outcomes

Control testing results should directly inform:

- Risk ratings 
- Assurance conclusions 
- Remediation priorities 
- Governance and escalation decisions 

---

## 📈 Reporting Outputs

Typical outputs include:

- Control test result summaries 
- Evidence logs 
- Findings registers 
- Remediation trackers 
- Executive summary reports 

---

## 🧠 Key Principles

- No assumption-based conclusions 
- Clear separation of design vs operating effectiveness 
- Evidence must support every assessment decision 
- All outputs must be reproducible and audit-defensible 
- Gaps should be explicitly recorded, not inferred 

---

## 📌 Use Cases

- Third-party supplier assurance 
- Internal security control testing 
- SOC 2 / ISO 27001 / NIST / NIS / PCI DSS / Cobit aligned assessments 
- Audit preparation and validation 
- Risk and compliance reporting
